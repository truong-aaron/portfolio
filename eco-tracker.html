Eco Tracker

visualize your daily carbon footprint

OVERVIEW



Team: Megan Dinh, Brendan Jew, Tatum Maston, Aaron Truong

Project Tag: UX/UI, Front-end, Web design

Duration: 9 weeks


Eco-Tracker is an application for users to easily track and visualize daily habits in order to be more eco-friendly.

It was created as part of COGS120 at UCSD, where my team had to develop an app from the ground-up over 9 weeks.





NEEDFINDING



Through interviews with students on campus, our group identified that the majority of young adults want to be more environmentally conscious. However, we noticed three recurring issues:


  1. Many don’t understand how their efforts are making a difference

  2. It’s confusing for them to understand how impactful daily tasks are, such as commuting by bike instead of driving

  3. Many simply don’t know how to be more environmentally conscious
    4.

With these in mind, our team audited existing emission trackers. We recognized that many were simply calculators that weren’t efficient for daily use and did little to motivate an eco-concious lifestyle.


Most of the existing “solutions” mainly provided rough annual estimates of total carbon emissions, which is too arbitrary for most users to conceptualize their impact.



ex. This calculator from the EPA doesn’t afford daily tracking and
visualization of impact beyond the metrics of CO2/year.



THE PROBLEM



From these audits and interviews findings, we recognized that current solutions and trackers weren’t working because their results were difficult to fully conceptualize and easily track varied tasks that differ day-to-day.


Additionally, maintaining motivation while seeing no tangible results is difficult> for any goal, but even more so with huge goals such as saving the Earth.


Users need a dynamic and understandable tracker that keeps up with their daily, everchanging lives.

STORYBOARD



To get a better understanding of how Eco-tracker could solve these problems, our team storyboarded daily scenarios where our app provided users ease in dynamic tracking and comprehending results in an everyday scenario.



This example also highlights how many folks who want to be more enviromentally friendly
can lose their motivation, simply because they have no idea if their efforts make an
impact–which Eco-track aims to fix!


Creating storyboards helped our team synthesize our previous research and findings to envision scenarios of Eco-tracker’s dynamic use & how it would address the opportunities from our needfinding.


WIREFRAME + PROTOTYPE



With an understanding of Eco-tracker’s design opportunities, our group began iterating with two paper prototypes that focused on different main functionalities.



Prototype 1



For prototype 1, the main functionality is for the users to add goals and reminders that will help them be more eco-friendly.

The main user flow for this prototype is for the user to easily set goals, track emissions, and work towards accomplishing those goals.



Prototype 2



For this second prototype, the emphasis is focused more towards tracking in general with the optional use of setting goals. As such, compared to the other design, this flow is centered around a home page that displays the daily emissions statistics.


From our research, users’ primary goals were to easily track and understand their environmental impact in a tangible manner.

Goals were nice to have, but having them as the focus didn’t address the core user needs. They wanted to visualize and understand that what they were doing was producing tangible results.

As a result, Prototype 2 was the one chosen to be further refined into a mid-fidelity wireframe (see below) as its statistics focused home page would best supported users in developing eco-friendly habits and address the opportunites discovered from our needfinding.




This design addressed the opportunities found in our needfinding by providing digestible data from users logged activities. It visualizes the impact that users have daily/monthly in categories so that they can easily ecognize it and make changes.

USER TESTING



For our initial round of testing, four random users from our main demographic (young, eco-conscious adults) participated. They were given a list of tasks to accomplish as we observered for any potential painpoints or issues.

The tests were conducted via mobile browsers, accessing our application that was hosted on Heroku.




Testing with a functional mobile prototype instead of laptops with Figma provided key insights into how we needed to adjust parts of our design to be mobile accessible.

Gestures that our team assumed were natural to do with a cursor didn’t always translate to productive results once we began testing with mobile users.

In this instance (pictured), we found that our buttons, specifically a sliding bar, were misplaced which caused issues such as entire windows being dragged.




Results from the testing were far from perfect. There were three main issues that our testers all experienced:


  1. Users couldn’t understand the signifiers used, such as the summary page and log icons on the navbar.

  2. Data figures on the summary page were too ambiguous and unmotivating to users, such as “L” for liters of carbon. Users additionally felt anxiety at how much they were wasting rather than saving.

  3. Users were confused whenever they were confronted with errors due to buggy interactions and couldn’t recover from them.



FINAL PROTOTYPE



In our final prototype, our team worked to address the issues brought up from our user testing sessions surrounding unclear signifiers, ambigious data presentation, & buggy interactions.





To resolve the majority of issues brought up from testing, our group focused the redesign on the home page, logging interactions, and menu bar.

Signifier (navbar) updates:

  • Summary icon has been updated to one more representative of its affordances
  • Removed unnecessary “Profile” tab from menu bar as a social network function was out of scope for the project
  • “Plus” icon was made larger and centered to increase ease of access to the main functionality of tracking


Clarified data:

  • Changed the ambiguous “L” unit to “lbs of CO2”, a more common unit of measurement that users are accustomed to


Increasing motivation:

  • Added modules that helped visualized users’ impacts in terms of positive environmental impacts such as “trees saved”, shifting tracking focus from carbon wasted to instead positive gains from habit changes.





Easier logging Users are able to input their exact mileage with a pop-up numpad rather than fidgeting with a slider that doesn’t offer as much control.

VALIDATING CHANGES



To validate if our new changes were effective, we conducted A/B testing with a large sample of 152 users.The participants were randomly assigned either the old or updated versions.


In order to measure the effectiveness of our changes, we tracked how many users were able to successfully log miles travelled & how long it took them do so using Google Analytics.





Of 115 successful task completions, the updated version (/viewAlt) had a higher rate of success of 20% more than the original (/commute).



Additionally, (/viewAlt) was also quicker on average to complete than (/commute) by almost 20 seconds!.


In summary, the results validated our updates as there was a statiscally higher and faster completion rate of the task than the original version.
